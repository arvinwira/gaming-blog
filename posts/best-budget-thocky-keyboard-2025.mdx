## Introduction
There is a sound captivating gamers, programmers, and typists across the internet. It is not the high pitched click of a generic office keyboard or the sharp clack of an old school mechanical board. It is a deep, resonant, and satisfyingly muted sound. It is the sound of "thock". This onomatopoetic term describes a keystroke that feels as good as it sounds, a full bodied report that evokes a sense of quality, precision, and substance. This auditory phenomenon has created a massive online community dedicated to chasing the perfect sound, with typing test videos garnering millions of views for their ASMR like appeal.   

&nbsp;

Just a few years ago, achieving this coveted sound was the exclusive domain of dedicated hobbyists. It required expensive custom kits, hours of painstaking labor lubricating individual switches, and a deep knowledge of aftermarket parts. The barrier to entry was high, both in cost and effort. The year 2025, however, represents a monumental paradigm shift in the mechanical keyboard market. The overwhelming demand for this specific sound profile, amplified by countless online communities and content creators, has not gone unnoticed by manufacturers. Companies like Aula, Keychron, and Epomaker have responded by engineering keyboards that deliver a near premium acoustic experience right out of the box, often for less than $100. What was once a luxury feature is now a competitive baseline, a true democratization of thock.   

&nbsp;

This article serves as the definitive guide to navigating this exciting new landscape. It will not simply list popular products. Instead, it will demystify the science behind the sound, breaking down the essential components that work in concert to create that perfect thock. Armed with this knowledge, readers will be able to critically evaluate any keyboard and make a confident, informed decision, finding the perfect board that delivers that deeply satisfying keystroke without emptying their wallet.

## The Anatomy of Thock: Deconstructing the Deepest Keyboard Sound
The term "thock" is an attempt to capture a specific auditory experience. It describes a sound that is deep, full, and muted, standing in stark contrast to the sharper, higher frequency sounds of "clack" or "click". While subjective, the community generally agrees that a thocky sound signature has a lower pitch and a soft, cushioned impact that suggests a solid, substantial build quality. Achieving this sound is not the result of a single magic component, but rather the synergistic interaction of the keyboard's entire construction. A mismatch in one area can completely alter the final sound profile. Understanding these five pillars is essential to identifying a truly thocky keyboard.   

## The Five Pillars of Thock
- Switches: The Heart of the Sound

The mechanical switch is the engine of the keyboard, and its design is the primary determinant of the sound profile. For a thocky sound, linear and tactile switches are overwhelmingly preferred. This is because they lack the internal "click jacket" found in clicky switches, which is designed to produce a sharp, high pitched sound. The materials used in the switch housing and stem also play a significant role, with higher quality, denser plastics contributing to a deeper tone. Furthermore, the practice of lubricating the switch, once a DIY-only modification, is now common from the factory. A precise application of lubricant on the stem and spring smooths the keypress, eliminating any scratchiness and dampening high frequency noises, which purifies the switch's inherent sound.   

&nbsp;

- Keycaps: Shaping the Resonance

Keycaps act as the resonating chamber for the sound produced by the switch. The material, thickness, and profile of the keycap all have a dramatic effect on the final acoustics. Polybutylene terephthalate, or PBT, is the preferred material for a thocky build. It is denser and thicker than the more common Acrylonitrile Butadiene Styrene, or ABS, plastic, resulting in a deeper sound. Keycap thickness is equally critical. Thicker keycap walls create a larger internal cavity, which allows lower frequency sounds to resonate more fully, contributing to a deeper, more substantial tone. Finally, certain keycap profiles, such as SA or Cherry, are known for their thicker construction and rounded shapes, which further enhance this full, resonant sound.   

&nbsp;

- Plate Material: The Keyboard's Tuning Fork

The plate is the component that holds the switches in place above the printed circuit board (PCB). Its material and stiffness act like a tuning fork for the entire keyboard, fundamentally influencing the frequency of the sound. Stiffer materials, such as steel, carbon fiber, or aluminum, tend to transfer vibrations more directly, resulting in a higher pitched, sharper sound often described as "clacky". To achieve a thocky profile, softer and more flexible materials are ideal. Polycarbonate (PC) and POM are popular choices in the enthusiast community because they absorb more of the high frequency vibrations, leading to a duller, lower pitched, and deeper sound upon bottom out.   

&nbsp;

- Mounting Style: The Gasket Revolution

How the plate and PCB assembly is mounted into the keyboard case has a profound impact on both feel and sound. The innovation that has defined the modern thocky keyboard is the gasket mount. In this design, strips of soft, flexible material like silicone or poron are placed between the plate and the keyboard case. This effectively isolates the typing assembly, allowing it to flex slightly under pressure and preventing harsh vibrations from transferring directly into the case. This cushioning effect creates a softer typing experience and, crucially, a more muted and deeper sound profile, making it an essential ingredient for achieving a clean thock.   

&nbsp;

- Case and Dampening: Eliminating Unwanted Noise

The keyboard's case is the final piece of the acoustic puzzle. Its material, whether plastic or aluminum, and its internal geometry create a space where sound can echo and reverberate. An empty, hollow case will produce a cheap, "plasticky" sound regardless of the quality of the other components. To combat this, modern keyboards are filled with sound dampening materials. Layers of silicone and poron foam are strategically placed between the plate and PCB, and below the PCB, to absorb stray sound waves and eliminate any hollowness. This meticulous process of filling empty space ensures that the only sound that reaches the ear is the pure, deep thock of the switch and keycap, without any unwanted resonance or ping.   
 
## Three Key Mods for Deeper Sound
### The Tape Mod
This is one of the most popular and effective mods for deepening a keyboard's sound. It involves applying two to three layers of painter's tape or masking tape to the back of the PCB. The tape acts as a dampener, absorbing high frequency sounds and reflecting lower frequencies, which results in a deeper, poppier, and more muted sound signature. It is a simple, reversible, and highly effective way to tune the acoustics. A critical safety warning must be observed: this mod should never be performed on a wireless keyboard where the battery is located directly against the PCB, as the tape could potentially cause a short circuit and a fire hazard.   

### The Foam Mod
Building on the principles of acoustic dampening, adding extra foam inside the keyboard's case is a surefire way to eliminate any remaining hollowness or echo. A thin layer of PE foam, the type often used as packing material, can be placed between the plate and the PCB to create a creamier sound. Additionally, if there is any empty space in the bottom of the case, filling it with denser case foam or even silicone can further absorb unwanted resonance, leading to a cleaner and more substantial sound.

### Switch Lubing Primer
For those willing to take the next step in the hobby, hand lubricating the switches is a highly rewarding process. It involves carefully disassembling each switch and applying a thin layer of specialized lubricant, like Krytox 205g0, to the sides of the stem and the spring. This process has two main benefits. First, it reduces friction, making the keypress significantly smoother. Second, it eliminates subtle noises like spring ping and the scratchy sound of plastic on plastic, resulting in a much cleaner, quieter, and more refined sound that allows the switch's true acoustic properties to shine through.
